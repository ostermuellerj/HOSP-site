The Emerald Ash Borer


The Emerald Ash Borer (EAB) is a beetle that raises major concerns to the safety of Ash trees in the Park. In the early summer, EAB implant their eggs in the bark of Ash trees where their larvae chew extensive channels underneath the bark throughout the fall. In the spring, EAB emerge from the tree leaving small D-shaped exit holes. EAB-ridden Ash trees die within one to four years of being infested3. Although EAB have yet to arrive in the Park, there have been confirmed sightings of the beetle less than 15 miles east of Hot Springs. As a result, one facet of this project is to monitor for the presence of EAB in the Park. To initiate this process, our group identified 17 Ash trees along the eastern edge of the Park (see locations on Map page). In the spring, when EAB are due to emerge, these trees will be examined to determine whether or not they are infested. If they are infested, approriate steps will be taken to assure the safety of the trees.
